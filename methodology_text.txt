GB Green Energy Investment Prioritisation Tool
Phase 1: Methodology Specification
Version 1.0  |  June 2026
1. Research Question
Which distribution network zones in Great Britain represent the highest-value targets for green energy investment, when modelled across renewable generation potential, existing grid constraints, demand growth signals, and socioeconomic vulnerability?
This question has practical relevance. The transition to net zero requires substantial capital allocation to electricity distribution infrastructure, yet no publicly accessible tool synthesises the available open data into a single, transparent, zone-level investability assessment. DNO licence areas vary substantially in their readiness to absorb and export renewable energy; this project attempts to make that variation legible.
2. Scope and Geographic Unit
The unit of analysis is the Distribution Network Operator (DNO) licence area. There are 14 DNO licence areas across Great Britain, operated by six licensed companies. This level of granularity is appropriate for three reasons:
It maps directly to available Ofgem performance and investment data, which is published at DNO level under the RIIO-ED2 price control framework.
DNO boundaries correspond to real regulatory and operational decision-making units, meaning scores are actionable rather than illustrative.
The number of zones (14) is large enough to produce meaningful rankings but small enough to be tractable for a first-pass MCDA model.
Sub-DNO analysis (e.g. at primary substation or grid supply point level) is identified as a direction for future work but is out of scope for this iteration due to data availability constraints.
3. Scoring Framework
The scoring model follows a Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) approach, specifically a Weighted Additive model (WADD). This method is selected over more complex alternatives (e.g. TOPSIS, ELECTRE, AHP) on the grounds of transparency, reproducibility, and interpretability for a policy-facing audience. As Wang et al. (2009) note in their survey of MCDA applications in sustainable energy, equal or simple weighted sum approaches remain the most widely used in practice precisely because stakeholders can interrogate and challenge them.
The composite score for each DNO zone is computed as:
Score(z) = w₁·D₁(z) + w₂·D₂(z) + w₃·D₃(z) + w₄·D₄(z) + w₅·D₅(z)
Where D₁–D₅ are normalised dimension scores (0–1 scale) and w₁–w₅ are user-configurable weights that sum to 1. All normalisation uses min-max scaling across the 14 zones.

6. Normalisation Approach
All dimension scores are normalised to a 0–1 range using min-max scaling before weighting:
D_norm = (x – x_min) / (x_max – x_min)
For inverse dimensions (where lower raw values indicate better investability, such as constraint severity), the formula is reversed:
D_norm = 1 – (x – x_min) / (x_max – x_min)
All transformation decisions will be documented in the pipeline code and referenced in Section 4 of the final report. This ensures full reproducibility.

8. Known Limitations
The following limitations are acknowledged upfront and will be discussed in the report:
DNO zone boundaries do not correspond to Local Authority or ONS geographies, requiring spatial aggregation of deprivation data that introduces approximation error.
RIIO-ED2 data reflects regulatory allowances, not real-time network conditions. Actual headroom may differ from reported figures.
The model is static: it scores zones at a point in time rather than modelling dynamic change. A time-series extension is identified as future work.
Equal treatment of DNO zones ignores their differing sizes and customer numbers. A population-weighted variant is noted as an extension.
The MCDA weighted additive approach assumes linear preferences and no interaction effects between dimensions. More sophisticated methods (e.g. AHP, TOPSIS) could address this at the cost of transparency.
